Who Owns Gold & Bitcoin

Individual (private-person) holdings by country, and how concentrated ownership is within each country — top vs. the rest. Gold reaches back ~40 years; Bitcoin since it began.

The shape of ownership

Two stores of value, two very different ownership structures. Gold is an ancient, broadly-held asset — especially as jewellery in cultural-gold economies. Bitcoin is young and, even after the ETF era, far more concentrated. Both are most concentrated exactly where wealth is.

    Gold — individual holdings & within-country concentration

    In cultural-gold economies (India, Vietnam, Türkiye) gold is the most democratic store of value there is — the poor hold a larger share of their wealth in it than the rich do. In investment-gold economies (US, Germany, Switzerland) it is concentrated among the affluent. Gold's share of wealth falls at the very top in both, so gold is always less top-1%-concentrated than wealth itself.

    Methodology & sources

    Every number is produced by a reproducible script; this page only displays them. Confidence is graded A (authoritative) / B (good) / C (weak/modeled).

    Gold — levels

    Private/household gold stock by country (jewellery + bars/coins), excluding central banks. Order-of-magnitude estimates from the World Gold Council / Metals Focus and national surveys (Steinbeis/Reisebank for Germany; WGC for India/Türkiye). Foreign-vaulted gold is not attributed to the host country. Valued at the World Bank annual gold price.

    Gold — within-country concentration

    A two-archetype intensity model: gold's share of wealth declines with wealth in cultural-gold economies (directionally consistent with the RBI Household Finance Committee — gold ≈ 20% of the poorest Indian household's wealth → ~2% of the richest's) and is mass-affluent-hump-shaped in investment-gold economies (calibrated to the US SCF). It is applied to each country's net-wealth distribution from the World Inequality Database (WID.world). The result is fundamentally driven by the wealth distribution; the gold-culture archetype (a coarse cultural / mixed / investment class) only tilts it — so "India broad / US concentrated" is a wealth-distribution result with a gold tilt, not a finely-tuned gold-culture model. India is a directional proxy, not fit to gold-by-decile microdata (India's AIDIS survey has excluded bullion since 2002). The US is shown from the SCF directly. History (40y) re-applies the model to each year's WID wealth shares. The global gold Gini (~0.61) is a modeled lower bound, not a measured value.

    Bitcoin — ownership & concentration

    On-chain supply distribution from BitInfoCharts. The entity waterfall removes lost coins (Chainalysis/Glassnode; Satoshi ~1.1M via the Patoshi pattern), government, corporate-treasury and miner coins; exchange- and ETF-held coins remain individually owned (custodial/indirect). Because ~76% of ETF AUM is institutional (Banque de France 13F), natural-person ownership is a range (~68–72%), not a point estimate. Individual concentration is anchored to Makarov & Schoar (NBER 29396 / AER 2023) and, for the US, the SCF (crypto = misc-asset type 85 = all crypto, not BTC alone). Country attribution is modeled (grade C, indicative) from ownership-rate surveys (Triple-A, Fed SHED, Bank of Canada, UK FCA) and cross-checked against the Chainalysis adoption index — China is the most uncertain (post-2021 trading ban; its rate is set low and is the single most sensitive assumption).

    Key caveats

    • Gold tonnages are estimates (±20–40%); no country runs a gold census.
    • Bitcoin country attribution has no on-chain ground truth — treat as indicative.
    • US SCF captures self-reported holdings only (levels understated); the concentration ratios are the robust output.
    • SCF "metals" combines gold+silver in the public file; it is investment bullion/coins, not jewellery.
